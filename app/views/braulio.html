Braulio Carrillo National Park

Established in April 15 th 1978, the park was formed as an agreement with environmental lobbyists to limit and prevent development of the area after the construction of the road to Limon a major road linking the capital to Caribbean cargo ports. The park was named in honor of former Costa Rican president Braulio Carrillo, (1837 to 1842) whose administration had helped foster the nation's early agricultural economy and had built the first road linking San José to the Caribbean coast.

Is part of the Central Volcanic Mountain Range, located on the eastern edge of San José, with easy access from the road to Limón, which bisects the park running roughly east-west.

With an extension of 47.583 hectares (117.842 acres) and with an average of 4.500 mm of rain per year, the park is home of around 6.000 species of vascular plants, 515 species of birds and 135 of mammals. Thousands of insects and mushrooms also reptiles, amphibious and butterflies are abundant.

The park is extremely important for the protection of aquifers, which provide mostly of the water to the Central Valley where 50% of the population inhabit, around 2.5 millions.

Contains many notable geological features, such as the Barba Volcano with three crater lakes: Barva, Danta and Copey. Cerro Chompipe, Cerro Cacho Negro, Hondura River, Patria River and the famous mineral-discolored Sucio (Dirty) River, located exactly where this river join the Hondura river with clear water. Obtained its name due to the sulfur origin or the water, which start at the highest part of Irazú volcano (active one), and gives the water the yellow intense color. The confluence of both rivers is the vertex of the provinces of San José, Cartago and Limón.

The park varied altitude from nearly 6.000 feet, from the highest to lowest points. Ranging from high altitude cloud forest to lowlands rainforest, it maintains one of the highest levels of biodiversity. Mostly is covered with old growth forest.
